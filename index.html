Bankruptcy Discharge Bars

A plain-language guide to the waiting periods that prevent debtors from receiving a bankruptcy discharge -- even if they're allowed to file. Covers Sections 727(a)(8), 727(a)(9), and 1328(f).

What Is a Discharge Bar?

A discharge bar is a statutory rule that prevents a debtor from receiving a discharge in a new bankruptcy case because they received a discharge too recently. The key distinction: a discharge bar is not the same as a filing bar.

Under Section 109(g), certain debtors are prohibited from filing a new case at all. A discharge bar is different -- the debtor can file a new case, and they may even receive the benefit of the automatic stay, but the court will not grant a discharge at the end of the case.

This means a debtor who files during a discharge bar period may go through the entire bankruptcy process -- paying attorney fees, attending the 341 meeting, making plan payments -- and still walk away without the debt relief they were seeking. Understanding these waiting periods before filing is critical.

Filing during a discharge bar period is one of the most common -- and most expensive -- mistakes in consumer bankruptcy. Debtors pay filing fees and attorney fees for a case that cannot result in a discharge. Some attorneys file these cases anyway, collecting fees for work that cannot succeed.

The Four Discharge Bar Periods

Federal law establishes four distinct waiting periods based on the combination of the prior case chapter and the new case chapter:

Prior Case New Case Wait Period Statute
Chapter 7 Chapter 7 8 years 727(a)(8)
Chapter 13 Chapter 7 6 years* 727(a)(9)
Chapter 7 Chapter 13 4 years 1328(f)(1)
Chapter 13 Chapter 13 2 years 1328(f)(2)

*The 6-year bar under 727(a)(9) has an important exception -- the "70% rule" discussed below.

These periods run from filing date to filing date, not from discharge date. This is a common point of confusion, and getting it wrong can mean filing too early and losing the right to a discharge.

How the Clock Works

All four discharge bar periods are measured from the date the prior case was filed to the date the new case is filed. This is a critical distinction that many debtors -- and some attorneys -- get wrong.

For example, if a debtor filed a Chapter 7 case on March 1, 2018, and received a discharge on June 15, 2018, the 8-year clock under 727(a)(8) runs from March 1, 2018 -- not from the June discharge date. The debtor would be eligible for a new Chapter 7 discharge on or after March 1, 2026.

Common misconception: Many people assume the clock runs from the discharge date. It does not. The statute says "the debtor has been granted a discharge under this section... in a case commenced within" the applicable period. The word "commenced" means the filing date of the prior case controls.

If the prior case was dismissed without a discharge, the discharge bar does not apply -- these statutes only trigger when a discharge was actually granted in the prior case. However, a dismissed case may still trigger the filing bar under Section 109(g), which is a separate issue.

The 70% Rule -- 727(a)(9) Exception

The 6-year bar from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7 under Section 727(a)(9) has an exception that the other discharge bars do not. A debtor who received a Chapter 13 discharge can obtain a Chapter 7 discharge within the 6-year window if both of the following are true:

  • The Chapter 13 plan paid at least 70% of allowed unsecured claims, and
  • The plan was proposed in good faith and represented the debtor's best effort.

Both prongs must be satisfied. A plan that paid 70% but was not proposed in good faith will not qualify. In practice, most Chapter 13 plans pay far less than 70% to unsecured creditors, so this exception applies relatively rarely.

The burden of proof is on the debtor to establish that the 70% threshold was met and that the plan was proposed in good faith. Debtors should obtain a copy of the Chapter 13 trustee's final report from the prior case to document the actual distribution percentage.

Discharge Bars vs. Filing Bars

It is important to distinguish between discharge bars and filing bars, because they have very different consequences:

Feature Discharge Bar (727(a)(8)/(9), 1328(f)) Filing Bar (109(g))
Can file new case? Yes No
Automatic stay? Yes (may be limited under 362(c)) N/A -- cannot file
Discharge available? No N/A -- cannot file
Triggered by Prior discharge Prior dismissal
Wait period 2, 4, 6, or 8 years 180 days

A debtor who files during a discharge bar period may still benefit from the automatic stay to stop a foreclosure or repossession -- but without a discharge, the underlying debt remains. Filing solely for the stay without discharge eligibility can also raise issues under Section 362(c), which limits the stay for repeat filers.
